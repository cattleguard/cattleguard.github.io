Professional Development

Ladder Safety

Published Sun, Sep 22, 2019 by Tony Towry

As I was listening to Go Time podcast ep. 99 - Hiring and nurturing junior developers on the way into the office and noting the parallels to the “cyber-security talent shortage”, I kept thinking about the inconvenient truth of how and why hiring junior talent isn’t incentivized.

I think what we’re really missing here is transaction cost (economics), time horizon (economics), and ability to architect expectations for career progression holistically.

One of the hosts of Go Time dropped some truth when he, to paraphrase, said “headcount is headcount” and they’d rather have the senior (10X Full-stack Unicorn).

I’ve grown peppers from seed, and I’ve grown peppers from small plants that came from a greenhouse. You end up putting a lot more energy into the seeds. Ask me which one ends up producing more in our limited growing season?

Can we extend the growing season?

Having more than a handful of jobs in a decade isn’t unusual. When we look at our list of projects and the knowledge, skills, and abilities (KSAs) required to extract value, we’re looking at programs and projects that run long and personnel that operate like vendors and move on in twelve to thirty-six months.

Why? It makes sense for the employee. In most cases they’ll never see pay bumps at their current employer, like they will when they change jobs.

If you’re growing talent, you better be damn good at retaining it.
